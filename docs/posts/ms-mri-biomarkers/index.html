MRI is the most important tool for diagnosing, monitoring, and assessing treatment response in MS. But standard T2-weighted imaging lacks pathologic specificity — plenty of conditions mimic MS on conventional sequences, and counting white spots only gets you so far. This post covers both conventional and emerging MRI biomarkers, and what actually changed in the 2024 revised McDonald criteria.

Personal notes based on: Lim TR et al. Conventional and Emerging MRI Biomarkers of Multiple Sclerosis. RadioGraphics 2026;46(3):e250069. https://doi.org/10.1148/rg.250069

The MS Disease Spectrum

MS is not a single entity but a spectrum of clinical phenotypes — and the labels matter because they carry different prognostic and therapeutic implications. - RIS (radiologically isolated syndrome) is incidental demyelinating lesions on MRI with no clinical symptoms; up to 50% convert to clinical MS within 10 years. - CIS (clinically isolated syndrome) is the first clinical demyelinating event, not yet meeting full criteria. - RRMS covers the relapsing-remitting course that accounts for ~85% of cases — alternating relapses and remissions with stable function between. - PPMS (~15%) is continuous progression from onset without relapses. - SPMS is what RRMS eventually becomes in many patients: gradual worsening after an initial relapsing course.

These are better understood as a continuous spectrum than as discrete disease categories, driven by overlapping mechanisms — acute inflammation, chronic compartmentalised inflammation, remyelination failure, and neurodegeneration — running in parallel at different rates.

The Diagnostic Framework: DIS and DIT

MS diagnosis rests on two pillars — dissemination in space (DIS) and dissemination in time (DIT) — which together establish that the disease is chronic and multifocal. Neither alone is sufficient. DIS without DIT could be a single monophasic event like ADEM; DIT without DIS could be a recurrent focal condition. - DIS requires T2-hyperintense lesions in at least two of five CNS topographies: periventricular, cortical or juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord, and — added in the 2024 criteria — optic nerve. - DIT is satisfied by either a new T2 or gadolinium-enhancing lesion on follow-up MRI, or the simultaneous presence of an enhancing and a non-enhancing lesion on a single scan. That second option matters: it allows DIT diagnosis at the very first visit, without waiting for a follow-up scan to show something new.

Conventional MRI Biomarkers

T2-Hyperintense Lesions

MS inflammation begins around post-capillary venules, and the shape of the resulting lesion is determined by the orientation of the culprit medullary vein. Ovoid lesions — the most common — spread elliptically along the vein. Dawson fingers are the pathognomonic periventricular variant: lesions oriented perpendicular to the lateral ventricles on sagittal FLAIR, directly tracing the medullary veins. Triangular or U-shaped lesions appear when the process reaches the ependymal surface or cortical boundary.

Typical locations: periventricular (directly contacting the ventricles), juxtacortical or leukocortical, infratentorial (brainstem, cerebellar peduncles, floor of the fourth ventricle), and spinal cord (short segments, peripheral, usually cervical). T2 lesion burden correlates only moderately with clinical disability — this disconnect is well established and is exactly why emerging biomarkers matter.

T1-Hypointense Lesions (“Black Holes”)

Acutely, T1 hypointensity can reflect oedema or acute demyelination and may resolve. When it persists, it represents irreversible neuroaxonal loss with reactive gliosis — the structural end-stage of a lesion. Black hole burden tracks with clinical disability and brain atrophy in a way that T2 lesion count simply does not.

Gadolinium-Enhancing Lesions

Enhancement signals acute blood-brain barrier breakdown and active inflammation. Typically nodular or open-ring in pattern (opening toward the ventricles or cortex), it lasts 2–8 weeks and marks current disease activity. Persistent enhancement beyond 3 months should prompt serious consideration of an alternative diagnosis. Enhancing lesions predict future relapses and disability in RRMS; new spinal cord lesions add prognostic weight even in patients who are clinically stable.

What Changed in 2024: The Revised McDonald Criteria

The headline update is the formal incorporation of emerging biomarkers — CVS and PRLs — into the diagnostic framework. Key changes from 2017:

What changed 2017 2024
DIS topographies 4 5 (optic nerve added)
Single-topography diagnosis Not possible Possible with CSF+ and CVS+ or PRL+, or DIT and CVS+ or PRL+
RIS Not MS MS if DIS + DIT, or CSF+, or CVS+
PPMS Required brain lesions Two or more spinal cord lesions sufficient for DIS

The single-topography scenario is the one that most changes daily practice. A patient with a typical clinical presentation and lesions in only one CNS region can now be diagnosed with MS — provided they have CSF positive for oligoclonal bands or kappa free light chains and either a positive central vein sign or a paramagnetic rim lesion. Earlier diagnosis, earlier treatment, fewer diagnostic limbo patients.

Emerging MRI Biomarkers

Central Vein Sign (CVS)

The CVS is a thin hypointense line or dot visible centrally within an MS lesion on susceptibility-sensitive MRI (T2*-segEPI or SWI). It corresponds to a small medullary vein — the entry point of perivenular inflammation that is the pathologic hallmark of MS. The signal loss comes from deoxyhaemoglobin in venous blood causing local field inhomogeneities. The lesion must be ≥3 mm, and the vein visible in at least two perpendicular planes.

Sensitivity 95–100%, specificity 88–97% for MS over mimics including NMOSD, MOGAD, small vessel disease, and migraine. In clinical practice, select3 (≥3 CVS-positive lesions) or select6 (≥6 CVS-positive lesions) are the simplified thresholds; percentage-based approaches (≥40–50% of lesions CVS-positive) are more accurate but more time-consuming. One important nuance: fewer CVS-positive new lesions are seen in patients ≥40 years, which may prevent unnecessary treatment escalation in that group.

Paramagnetic Rim Lesions (PRLs)

PRLs are a subset of chronic active lesions (CALs) — lesions with ongoing, compartmentalised inflammation behind an intact blood-brain barrier. Histologically, they have a hypocellular, fully demyelinated core surrounded by a dense rim of iron-laden CD68-positive macrophages and activated microglia. On susceptibility-sensitive MRI, this iron rim appears as a hypointense rim at the outer edge of the lesion, continuous through ≥2/3 of the circumference. Unlike gadolinium enhancement, PRLs do not enhance — different biology, different sequence finding.

The spatial relationship to remember: the CVS sits at the centre of the lesion (the vein); the PRL rim sits at the edge (the macrophages). Same sequence, opposite ends of the lesion, completely different pathology.

Prevalence: 50–60% of MS patients have at least one PRL. Specificity: 87–100% for MS. Higher PRL counts predict progressive phenotypes, worse disability, cognitive impairment, and faster progression — making them one of the more powerful prognostic tools now entering clinical use.

Chronic Active Lesions and Slowly Expanding Lesions (SELs)

CALs encompass the broader category of mixed active and inactive lesions with ongoing tissue injury, including PRLs and the related entity of slowly expanding lesions (SELs) — non-enhancing T2 lesions that show slow, concentric longitudinal enlargement over 1–2 years on serial 3D T1 or T2 MRI. SELs and PRLs partially co-localise but represent different stages of the same underlying pathologic process. Higher SEL counts are associated with progressive MS and future disability worsening.

Cortical Lesions

MS is not purely a white matter disease. Cortical lesions have been a recognised topography since the 2017 criteria and are now well-established both pathologically and clinically. Four subtypes by location within the cortical laminae: - Type I (leukocortical) span the grey-white junction and are the easiest to detect (~40% visible on 3D FLAIR at 3T). - Type II (intracortical) lie entirely within the cortex and are rarely detected at clinical field strengths. - Type III (subpial) run along the pial surface in a band-like pattern and are nearly invisible at 1.5–3T (~5% detection). - Type IV extend the full cortical depth.

Subpial lesions are highly specific for MS — rarely seen in other conditions — topographically associated with meningeal lymphoid aggregates, and strongly linked to disability and cognitive impairment. 7T MRI dramatically improves cortical lesion detection and will likely become increasingly important as higher field strengths move toward clinical routine.

Leptomeningeal Enhancement

Focal high signal intensity in the subarachnoid space on delayed post-contrast 3D T2w-FLAIR, reflecting meningeal inflammatory infiltrates. Associated with cortical lesions, longer disease duration, greater disability, and cortical atrophy. Currently controversial as a clinical biomarker — associations are inconsistent across studies and the finding lacks MS-specificity.

Atrophy and Volumetric Measures

Neurodegeneration — axonal loss, neuronal death, gliosis — progresses independently of acute inflammation and is the main driver of irreversible disability. Grey matter atrophy correlates more strongly with clinical disability than white matter atrophy, because grey matter contains the neuronal cell bodies and synaptic connections that actually mediate function.

The key patterns: - Cortical atrophy is strongly associated with disease progression and SPMS conversion. - Thalamic atrophy is linked to both physical disability and cognitive impairment and is a risk factor for worsening. - Spinal cord atrophy (especially cervical) progresses faster than brain atrophy, is more pronounced in PPMS, and even small increases predict disability progression. - Choroid plexus enlargement is larger in MS than in healthy controls or NMOSD, and is associated with lesion load, atrophy, and cognitive dysfunction — a newer marker still being validated.

One clinical trap worth flagging: cerebral pseudoatrophy — apparent volume loss in the first 6–12 months after starting disease-modifying therapy or following a relapse. It reflects reduction of oedema, not true neurodegeneration, and can confound serial volumetric measurements if you’re not expecting it.

Poor Prognostic MRI Features at a Glance

  • Conventional: high T2 lesion burden, new T2 lesions, gadolinium-enhancing lesions, infratentorial lesions, spinal cord lesions at baseline.
  • Emerging: CALs, PRLs, SELs, cortical lesions.
  • Volumetric: whole-brain atrophy, cortical and thalamic atrophy, spinal cord atrophy, choroid plexus enlargement.

The Big Picture

MS imaging has moved well past counting T2 lesions. The current framework spans the full biological spectrum: perivenular origin (CVS), acute BBB breakdown (enhancement), chronic smouldering inflammation (PRLs, SELs), and irreversible tissue loss (black holes, atrophy). The 2024 McDonald criteria formalise this shift — incorporating CVS and PRLs not just as research tools but as diagnostic criteria, enabling earlier and more specific diagnosis in exactly the borderline cases where it matters most.

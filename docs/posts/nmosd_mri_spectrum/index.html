NMOSD and MS can look similar on conventional MRI. But their treatments are fundamentally different — and some MS-specific drugs can actively worsen NMOSD. Getting this distinction right is not academic: it directly determines patient outcomes. This post covers the imaging spectrum of NMOSD, the mechanistic logic behind its lesion distribution, and how to distinguish it from MS, MOGAD, and a range of non-demyelinating mimics.

Pathogenesis: NMOSD is an Astrocytopathy

The key antibody is anti-AQP4-IgG — an IgG autoantibody targeting aquaporin-4 water channels on astrocyte foot processes. What follows is a cascade that is worth tracing step by step, because it explains almost everything about how NMOSD looks on imaging: AQP4-IgG enters the CNS at areas of increased BBB permeability or via transcytosis through endothelial cells; it binds to AQP4 on astrocyte foot processes and triggers complement activation; complement increases BBB permeability and recruits leukocytes — predominantly neutrophils and eosinophils, not lymphocytes as in MS; leukocyte degranulation kills astrocytes; dying astrocytes release chemokines that attract macrophages; macrophages phagocytose myelin, and the cascade ends in oligodendrocyte and neuronal death.

The critical point: demyelination in NMOSD is secondary to astrocyte destruction. NMOSD is more accurately classified as an astrocytopathy. This explains why MS treatments targeting T-cell pathways are ineffective — or actively harmful — while treatments targeting B cells (anti-CD20), complement, or leukocyte infiltration are mechanistically rational.

Where AQP4 Lives — Predicting the Lesion Map

AQP4 is most densely expressed wherever astrocyte foot processes contact CSF or blood vessels: the subependymal surfaces lining the ventricles, subpial regions, circumventricular organs (no BBB — extremely high AQP4), optic nerves (bathed in CSF within their subarachnoid sheath), spinal cord central grey matter around the central canal, and the hypothalamus, area postrema, and brainstem.

This distribution predicts the NMOSD lesion map with remarkable accuracy. Learn where AQP4 is concentrated and the imaging map writes itself.

Imaging Findings by Location

Spinal Cord

The spinal cord is the most typical NMOSD target, and it announces itself clearly.

  • LETM (longitudinally extensive transverse myelitis) involves ≥3 vertebral segments, is centrally placed along the central canal grey matter, and covers >50% of the cord cross-section on axial images — with the cervicothoracic segments most commonly affected.
  • Bright spotty lesions (BSL) are focal areas of extreme T2 hyperintensity on axial imaging — brighter than CSF — without flow void effects, reflecting necrosis and microcystic change from severe astrocyte destruction.
  • Dark T1 lesions in the same areas represent necrosis and cavitation, more severe than the gliosis-based black holes of MS. The BSL-plus-dark T1 pairing is a useful double sign.
  • Lens-shaped enhancement on sagittal contrast T1 encases the bright spotty lesion in a lenticular ring — distinct from the patchy or nodular MS cord enhancement.

One important caveat: NMOSD can present with cord lesions spanning fewer than 3 segments in 14.5% of initial presentations. Short transverse myelitis does not exclude NMOSD — AQP4-IgG testing is warranted regardless of lesion length when the clinical picture fits.

When cervical cord is involved, upward extension to the area postrema is commonly observed.

Area Postrema — The Clinically Surprising Target

The area postrema sits in the dorsal medulla at the floor of the fourth ventricle. It’s a circumventricular organ with no blood-brain barrier and extremely high AQP4 expression — which makes it a primary NMOSD target. The clinical presentation is memorable once you know it: intractable hiccups, nausea, and vomiting lasting days to weeks, unresponsive to antiemetics. This is a core clinical characteristic of NMOSD under the 2015 IPND criteria and is highly specific when present.

The trap: these patients typically land in gastroenterology, diagnosed with functional dyspepsia or gastritis, and the neurologic cause is missed entirely. Any patient with unexplained intractable hiccups and vomiting deserves brain MRI with specific attention to the dorsal medulla.

Optic Nerve

NMOSD optic neuritis is bilateral, longitudinally extensive (>half the optic nerve length), and targets the posterior optic nerve and chiasm — the intracranial portion bathed in CSF where AQP4 is concentrated. Acutely: T2 hyperintensity and T1 gadolinium enhancement. Chronically: optic nerve atrophy. The bilateral and posterior-predominant pattern distinguishes it from MS, which is typically unilateral and anterior.

Brain

Brain lesions occur in 24–89% of NMOSD patients and are mostly clinically silent. The patterns follow AQP4 distribution.

  • The corpus callosum is affected at its ependymal surface rather than at the callosal-septal interface (as in MS): on sagittal FLAIR this produces heterogeneous, oedematous hyperintensity — the marbled pattern; on axial FLAIR, periependymal lesions along both lateral ventricles create the arch bridge appearance.
  • Periependymal lesions follow the lining of the lateral, third, and fourth ventricles; hypothalamic involvement can cause narcolepsy, anorexia, and temperature dysregulation.
  • The trident sign appears when a corticospinal tract lesion in the posterior limb of the internal capsule generates vasogenic oedema extending into the adjacent temporal lobe.
  • Cortical lesions are a red flag — their presence should prompt reconsideration toward MS, MOGAD, or vasculitis.

Enhancement Patterns

NMOSD enhances in two characteristic ways that differ from MS: cloud-like enhancement (patchy, inhomogeneous, poorly defined margins) and pencil-thin periependymal enhancement (thin linear enhancement along the ventricular surfaces). Open-ring enhancement, by contrast, strongly favours MS — it is rarely seen in NMOSD.

Optic nerve segment Posterior + chiasm Anterior, intraorbital Anterior, intraorbital

Length Long (>½ nerve) Long Short
Chiasm Commonly involved Rarely involved Rarely involved
Enhancement Variable Perineural (tram-track) Variable
Spinal cord LETM, central, cervicothoracic LETM, H-sign, conus/thoracolumbar Short, peripheral, cervical
Brain Periependymal, corticospinal tract Deep grey matter, cerebellum, ADEM-like Periventricular (Dawson fingers), juxtacortical
Prognosis Poor, relapsing Often monophasic, favourable Variable

The perineural enhancement in MOGAD — a rim of gadolinium around the optic nerve sheath — reflects the accessibility of MOG on the outer myelin surface to antibodies in the surrounding orbital fat. Absent in NMOSD. That tram-track appearance on fat-saturated post-contrast sequences is one of the more distinctive signs in this entire group.

Key DDx Mimics

  • Subacute combined degeneration (B12 deficiency): LETM with selective dorsal and lateral column involvement on axial cord imaging. No vasogenic oedema, minimal enhancement. Clinical context — B12 deficiency, sensory ataxia, peripheral neuropathy — is the key.
  • Acute spinal cord infarct: sudden onset, anterior spinal artery distribution. Classic axial appearance: “owl eyes” or “snake eyes” — bilateral anterior horn involvement. Restricted diffusion on DWI is the discriminator.
  • Susac syndrome: rare small-vessel disease with corpus callosum involvement at its central portion (not the ependymal surface as in NMOSD), producing small linear “snowball” lesions on FLAIR. The triad of sensorineural hearing loss, retinal artery occlusions, and encephalopathy is the clinical tell.
  • CLIPPERS: “peppering” gadolinium enhancement in the pons, middle cerebellar peduncles, and cerebellum. Steroid-responsive. The infratentorial predilection distinguishes it from NMOSD.
  • CMV ventriculitis: pencil-thin ependymal enhancement that closely mimics NMOSD periependymal patterns. The context makes the diagnosis: immunocompromised patient (HIV, transplant). Restricted diffusion and intraventricular debris support ventriculitis over NMOSD.
  • Sarcoidosis: cranial nerve and optic nerve thickening, nodular enhancement near the hypothalamus and visual pathways, leptomeningeal involvement. Known systemic sarcoidosis should always put this on the list.
  • GFAP astrocytopathy: shares longitudinally extensive myelitis, bilateral optic involvement, and periependymal enhancement with NMOSD. The discriminator is the enhancement pattern: GFAP produces radial perivascular enhancement perpendicular to the ventricles, while NMOSD produces periependymal or cloud-like enhancement. Optic involvement differs too — GFAP causes papillitis (disc oedema), NMOSD targets the posterior optic nerve and chiasm. CSF GFAP-IgG and AQP4-IgG serology are the definitive tests.

The Big Picture

NMOSD lesions follow AQP4 — wherever astrocyte foot processes contact CSF or blood vessels, that’s where the disease attacks. Once that principle is internalised, the imaging map becomes predictable: posterior optic nerve and chiasm, long central cord lesions with bright spotty signs, periependymal brain lesions, area postrema. The key discriminators from MS come down to three things: location (posterior vs anterior optic nerve; central vs peripheral cord; periependymal vs periventricular brain), lesion character (LETM vs short segment; bright spotty signs vs Dawson fingers), and clinical context (bilateral optic neuritis plus myelitis; intractable hiccups as presenting symptom). When the distinction is unclear, AQP4-IgG serology is the decisive test.

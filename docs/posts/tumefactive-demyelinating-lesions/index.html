Some demyelinating lesions are easy to spot: small, periventricular, ovoid, oriented along the medullary veins. But a subset are large, oedematous, and enhancing — and on first pass look indistinguishable from a brain tumour. These are tumefactive demyelinating lesions, and understanding them is as much about recognising their imaging fingerprints as it is about understanding why they look the way they do.

What Makes a Lesion “Tumefactive”?

The working definition is size — greater than 2 cm on T2-weighted imaging, with significant mass effect and surrounding oedema. In practice, smaller lesions (0.5–2 cm) can behave identically. The problem is not the size itself but the combination: a large demyelinating lesion plus mass effect plus contrast enhancement reads as a space-occupying lesion, and patients frequently end up having brain biopsies.

The reason these lesions enhance and exert mass effect comes directly from their pathophysiology. Tumefactive demyelination is an intense focal inflammatory process: activated T-cells and macrophages infiltrate the perivascular space, release cytokines and matrix metalloproteinases that break down BBB tight junctions, and drive vasogenic oedema. The resulting fluid accumulation and tissue swelling produce mass effect. Enhancement follows because contrast leaks through the disrupted barrier. This is the same mechanism as any active MS plaque — just scaled up considerably.

The Signature Imaging Feature: Open Ring Enhancement

The most specific imaging sign — reported specificity of 98–100% — is the open or incomplete ring of enhancement. The ring opens toward the grey matter (cortex or basal ganglia), and the enhancing arc faces the white matter.

The mechanism is elegant: the enhancing rim represents the active advancing front of demyelination, where the inflammatory infiltrate is pushing through tissue and breaking down the BBB as it goes. Demyelination preferentially advances through white matter, where myelinated axons and oligodendrocytes are abundant. When the front reaches grey matter, it stalls — fewer oligodendrocytes, sparser myelination — and BBB disruption stops at that interface. No advancing front, no enhancement. The ring stays open on the grey matter side.

The non-enhancing centre represents older, more quiescent inflammation — the front has already passed through, leaving demyelinated tissue but without active BBB breakdown.

Worth noting: the closed ring pattern (18% of tumefactive lesions) does not argue against demyelination. It simply means the advancing front encircled the lesion without a grey matter boundary to stop it. And beyond ring patterns, tumefactive lesions show a wide range of enhancement morphologies — homogeneous, heterogeneous, nodular, cotton-ball — which is itself a useful reminder that no single enhancement pattern makes or excludes the diagnosis.

DWI: Heterogeneous ADC as a Clue

The diffusion signal in tumefactive lesions is heterogeneous in a specific and mechanistically meaningful way: ADC is high centrally and low at the periphery.

Centrally, vasogenic oedema and myelin destruction produce abundant free water that diffuses easily — high ADC. At the rim, the advancing front is packed with macrophages and lymphocytes, whose dense cellularity restricts extracellular water movement — low ADC, analogous to restricted diffusion in highly cellular tumours. This peripheral restriction can evolve dynamically on serial MRI as the inflammatory front advances — a pattern not seen in abscesses or neoplasms, and one of the more useful features for distinguishing demyelination from tumour over time.

The Central Vein Sign in Tumefactive Lesions

In MS-associated tumefactive lesions, the central vein sign (CVS) — a hypointense linear vessel running centrally through the lesion on SWI or T2* — is present in 94% of periventricular and 84% of deep white matter tumefactive lesions. When more than 54% of white matter lesions on a single scan show the CVS, MS diagnostic accuracy reaches 94% (sensitivity 90%, specificity 100%). Finding a CVS within a large tumefactive-looking lesion is a strong argument for a demyelinating aetiology.

Advanced Imaging

MR spectroscopy shows an increased Cho/NAA ratio in tumefactive lesions, reflecting membrane phospholipid breakdown and macrophage activity. This overlaps with neoplasms (where elevated choline reflects rapid membrane synthesis in proliferating cells), and a Cho/NAA cutoff of >1.72–1.73 has been proposed to distinguish high-grade glioma from demyelination. In practice, this threshold has limited applicability: MRS is heavily influenced by pulse sequence, echo time, lesion age, and voxel placement, making centre-to-centre comparison unreliable.

FDG-PET shows glucose uptake in tumefactive lesions approximately equal to normal cortex — lower than most high-grade gliomas. Perfusion MRI results are mixed and not yet reliable enough to determine management.

Disease Spectrum

Tumefactive demyelination is not a disease in itself — it is a pattern that can occur across several immune-mediated conditions, each with distinctive features:

Entity Distinctive features
MS CVS present in ~90%; periventricular satellite lesions establish DIS; 22–70% of inaugural isolated tumefactive lesions convert to MS within 5 years
Baló concentric sclerosis Alternating T2-hyper and isointense rings (demyelinated vs spared myelin); now considered part of the MS spectrum; CSF oligoclonal band pattern differs from typical MS
Schilder disease Large bilateral symmetric lesions spanning centrum semiovale and corpus callosum; curvilinear T2-hypointense, enhancing, DWI-restricted active borders
NMOSD Absent or minimal enhancement (AQP4 antibodies spare the perivenular space longer); when present, cloud-like or incomplete peripheral ring; large lesion + no enhancement should raise suspicion
ADEM Predominantly paediatric, monophasic; mixed ADC (vasogenic + cytotoxic oedema within same lesion); multifocal FLAIR involvement of WM, corpus callosum, basal ganglia, thalami
DVA-associated TDL adjacent to a developmental venous anomaly; biopsy carries risk of venous infarction — avoid direct sampling near the DVA; CVS may point toward the anomaly

Management

Asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic lesions can be observed with follow-up MRI at 4–6 weeks. Symptomatic lesions are treated with high-dose IV corticosteroids as first line, with good response in most cases. Steroid-refractory disease is treated with plasma exchange, followed by cyclophosphamide or rituximab if plasma exchange fails. When tumefactive demyelination occurs in the context of established MS, disease-modifying therapy is indicated — with specific caution around fingolimod and natalizumab, both of which have been associated with triggering tumefactive demyelination.

The CNS lymphoma trap: steroids cause transient lysis of lymphoma cells, producing apparent clinical and imaging improvement that mimics a demyelinating response — and a subsequent biopsy may appear to show demyelination. If CNS lymphoma is seriously on the differential (immunocompromised patient, atypical presentation), biopsy should precede steroids.

GFAP astrocytopathy is a second steroid-sensitive mimic worth flagging: enhancement patterns overlapping with tumefactive demyelination have been reported, and a dramatic steroid response can produce premature diagnostic closure. When the clinical picture includes meningoencephalitis, bilateral papillitis, or a paraneoplastic context (ovarian teratoma in particular), CSF GFAP-IgG should be sent before attributing the lesion to demyelination.

Long-term outlook: relapses occur in ~17% of isolated tumefactive demyelination cases, and the majority of patients presenting with an inaugural isolated lesion will ultimately be diagnosed with MS over the following years. Long-term follow-up is essential regardless of initial apparent resolution.

The Big Picture

Tumefactive demyelinating lesions look like tumours, but their imaging fingerprints are mechanistically grounded: the open ring enhancement reflects an advancing inflammatory front that stalls at the grey matter boundary; the heterogeneous ADC pattern — high centrally, low at the rim — maps directly onto oedematous core versus densely cellular periphery; and the CVS, when present, anchors the lesion to its perivenular origin.

The most important diagnostic axis is the disease entity behind the pattern: MS is the most common and the CVS your best ally; NMOSD should be considered when a large lesion enhances minimally or not at all; DVA-association needs to be recognised before biopsy is attempted. And in any case where steroids are being considered, CNS lymphoma must first be weighed — because treating lymphoma like demyelination buys time the patient may not have.

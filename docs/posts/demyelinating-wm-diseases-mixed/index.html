Every white matter disease session eventually runs into the same problem: there are too many entities, and without a framework they blur together. Sarbu et al. 2016 is the paper that provides such a framework — a pathophysiologic classification of white matter diseases teaching us the why behind the what to help organize our clinical intuition.

Based on: Sarbu N, Shih RY, Jones RV, Horkayne-Szakaly I, Oleaga L, Smirniotopoulos JG. White Matter Diseases with Radiologic-Pathologic Correlation. RadioGraphics 2016;36:1426–1447. doi:10.1148/rg.2016160031

The Framework

White matter diseases divide first into two broad groups: demyelinating (secondary destruction of already-formed myelin) and dysmyelinating (primary abnormality of myelin formation — the leukodystrophies). Demyelinating diseases then subdivide into four mechanistic categories:

  • Autoimmune — perivenular inflammation attacking oligodendrocytes or astrocytes (MS, ADEM, NMOSD)
  • Infectious — direct pathogen-mediated myelin destruction (PML, HIV encephalopathy, Lyme)
  • Vascular — ischemic or inflammatory injury to white matter microvasculature (small-vessel disease, CADASIL, Susac)
  • Toxic-metabolic — osmotic, nutritional, or drug-mediated injury to myelin (osmotic myelinolysis, PRES, Wernicke)

This isn’t just taxonomy. Each category points at a different diagnostic workup: autoimmune → antibodies and CSF; infectious → serology, PCR, immune status; vascular → risk factors, vessel imaging, genetics; toxic-metabolic → drug and metabolic history. The imaging alone often can’t resolve the differential — but knowing which category you’re in immediately focuses the next step.

Autoimmune White Matter Diseases

MS, Tumefactive Demyelinating Lesion (TDL), Balo, Schilder

MS, TDL, Balo concentric sclerosis, and Schilder disease are covered in depth in other posts. The classification point worth reiterating: all are primary demyelinating diseases of unknown autoimmune etiology, characterized histologically by perivenular lymphocytic infiltration and demyelination with relative axon preservation. Balo’s onion-bulb concentric rings and TDL’s open-ring enhancement and low rCBV are their imaging signatures.

MS MRI follow-up (2020 update): monitoring frequency is driven by PML risk, not just disease activity. Patients on natalizumab >24 months, with positive JC serology, or on immunosuppression need MRI every 3–4 months — the window for catching early asymptomatic PML before it becomes clinically devastating. Low-risk patients scan annually; stable and asymptomatic for 1–2 years can scan less frequently.

NMOSD and MOGAD

The pathologic distinction is fundamental. AQP4+ NMOSD is an astrocytopathy: antibodies target aquaporin-4 on astrocyte endfeet concentrated at periependymal surfaces, explaining the tight anatomical specificity of its lesions. MOGAD is an oligodendrocytopathy: MOG sits on the outermost surface of the myelin sheath, fully exposed to circulating antibodies, with no anatomical constraint — lesions are more diffuse and not periependymally anchored.

Pathology Astrocytopathy Oligodendrocytopathy
Optic neuritis ~45%, often unilateral 60–74%, more bilateral; “dirty” intracranial fat; spares chiasm
Area postrema syndrome ~40% 6–15%
Transverse myelitis LETM, upper cord/cervicomedullary LETM, lower cord/conus (sphincter dysfunction)
Encephalitis Rare More common

Prognosis Worse, greater disability Better, functional return

The conus/lower cord preference of MOGAD is an imaging discriminator in practice: LETM centered on the conus with sphincter symptoms → think MOG-Ab first.

ADEM — Acute Disseminated Encephalomyelitis

ADEM is the entity most commonly confused with MS on first imaging, and it’s not intuitive — so it deserves careful unpacking.

The mechanism is molecular mimicry. An infection (50–75%, usually upper respiratory) or vaccination triggers an immune response that cross-reacts with myelin proteins — most importantly MOG (myelin oligodendrocyte glycoprotein), with anti-MOG IgG found in about 50% of cases. The histology is perivenular demyelination, identical to MS — but the trigger is exogenous, and the disease is monophasic in 90% of cases.

This monophasic nature has a direct imaging corollary: all lesions tend to be the same age and may all enhance simultaneously. In MS, lesions are always at different stages of evolution.

The lesion distribution reflects the more diffuse immune activation compared to MS: bilateral, large, rounded, poorly defined, involving both white and gray matter — deep nuclei, thalami, basal ganglia, and brainstem. Brainstem lesions are ventral midbrain, bilateral and symmetric with poorly defined margins (contrast MS: peripheral, asymmetric, well-defined). U-fibers are frequently involved.

The corpus callosum difference from MS is operationally important. MS lesions arise from the callososeptal interface, producing the classic subcallosal “dot-dash” on sagittal FLAIR. ADEM lesions arise from the periventricular white matter — they are larger, not anchored to the underside of the corpus callosum, and do not produce the Dawson finger morphology.

Course Multiphasic Monophasic (90%)

AHL (Hurst disease) is the fulminant hemorrhagic variant — pathologically similar to ADEM but with vascular injury causing hemorrhage. The single distinguishing imaging feature is hemorrhage, which must be present (T2* blooming), either diffusely or in a pseudotumoral pattern. Clinically: death within days to weeks from brainstem involvement.

Infectious White Matter Diseases

PML — Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy

JC virus (polyomavirus family) infects and lyses oligodendrocytes — which explains why PML is a white matter disease. Latent in healthy individuals, it reactivates under cellular immunodeficiency: HIV/AIDS (CD4 <100), lymphoproliferative disease, transplantation, and MS patients on natalizumab (which blocks lymphocyte trafficking into the CNS, removing immune surveillance).

The imaging follows from the target cell. Oligodendrocytes are concentrated in the peripheral white matter and subcortical U-fibers, so PML produces peripheral, asymmetric, confluent white matter lesions with U-fiber involvement. Because the underlying immune deficiency prevents an inflammatory response, there is classically no significant contrast enhancement and no mass effect. Exceptions: IRIS after antiretroviral initiation (immune reconstitution can trigger paradoxical enhancement), and natalizumab-associated PML when the drug is stopped.

HIV Encephalopathy

HIV targets microglia, not oligodendrocytes — leading to diffuse microglial activation and neurodegeneration rather than focal demyelination. The imaging reflects this: diffuse, bilateral, symmetric periventricular white matter signal change without mass effect or enhancement. Critically, brain atrophy is a hallmark — out of proportion to age. Occurs in 15–20% of AIDS patients despite antiretroviral therapy.

Lyme Disease

CNS involvement in 10–15% of patients. Imaging shows multifocal WMH with variable enhancement; leptomeningeal and cranial nerve enhancement (particularly the trigeminal nerve) is a distinguishing feature from MS. CSF shows pleocytosis with lymphocyte predominance; serology confirms.

Vascular White Matter Diseases

Small-vessel disease is the single most common cause of white matter disease — responsible for ~45% of dementias and ~20% of strokes. The paper classifies it into six types, but four have distinct imaging signatures worth knowing.

Arteriolosclerosis (Type 1)

Age- and hypertension-related deep perforator disease. WMH in deep white matter, basal nuclei, corona radiata. Key feature: U-fibers spared — they receive dual blood supply (cortical + deep penetrating arteries) and are protected from deep perforator ischemia. This is the most clinically useful discriminator from MS.

Cerebral Amyloid Angiopathy (Type 2)

β-amyloid deposits in the walls of cortical and leptomeningeal arteries. Imaging: peripheral (corticosubcortical) microbleeds on SWI, lobar hemorrhages, superficial siderosis. Occipital and frontal predominance. Definitive diagnosis requires postmortem; the modified Boston criteria allow radiologic diagnosis.

CADASIL (Type 3)

CADASIL (Cerebral Autosomal-Dominant Arteriopathy with Subcortical Infarcts and Leukoencephalopathy) is caused by a mutation in the NOTCH3 gene on chromosome 19, leading to granular osmiophilic material deposition around smooth muscle cells of leptomeningeal and perforating arteries.

The imaging follows a characteristic temporal course: temporal poles (early, often before symptoms — the pathognomonic finding), then posterior frontal-parietal-occipital, then external capsule involvement, eventually with microbleeds and lacunar infarcts. Temporal pole + external capsule WMH in a young patient with migraines and strokes = CADASIL until proven otherwise. Send NOTCH3 genetics; avoid angiography (high complication risk from arteriopathic vessels).

PACNS (Type 4 — Inflammatory)

Primary angiitis of the CNS is limited to brain and spinal cord, with granulomatous inflammation of small and medium leptomeningeal and cortical vessels. The imaging is nonspecific: multifocal cortical-subcortical infarcts, hemorrhages, leptomeningeal enhancement. The critical clinical pearl: a normal MRI makes PACNS unlikely — MRI is virtually always abnormal when PACNS is present. Definitive diagnosis requires brain biopsy.

Susac Syndrome (Type 4 — Inflammatory)

Susac is a rare autoimmune endotheliopathy targeting small neural vessels. The clinical triad — the “E” syndrome — is the anchor: Ear (sensorineural hearing loss), Eye (visual loss from branch retinal artery occlusion), Encephalopathy. Ophthalmoscopy demonstrating retinal artery occlusions is a cornerstone of diagnosis.

The brain imaging shows microinfarcts in different phases, periventricular WM and deep gray matter involvement, and the characteristic corpus callosum finding: central full-thickness “snowball” lesions involving the body, not arising from the callososeptal interface. The internal capsule shows a “string of pearls” pattern from the microinfarcts.

Toxic-Metabolic White Matter Diseases

Osmotic Myelinolysis

Caused by rapid correction of hyponatremia (or, less commonly, rapid osmotic shifts in other contexts). In 65% of cases: central pontine T2 hyperintensity with peripheral sparing — the pontine fiber tracts are densely packed and maximally vulnerable. In one-third of cases extrapontine: thalami, basal nuclei, subcortical white matter. Diffusion restriction in the acute phase. The imaging often lags the clinical event by 1–2 weeks.

PRES — Posterior Reversible Encephalopathy Syndrome

Triggered by hypertensive crisis, preeclampsia/eclampsia, or cytotoxic drugs. The mechanism is failure of cerebrovascular autoregulation → breakthrough hyperperfusion → vasogenic edema — not vasospasm, not ischemia. Because the posterior circulation has less sympathetic innervation, it autoregulates less effectively against pressure surges, explaining the posterior (parieto-occipital) predominance.

The word “reversible” is conditional — rapid treatment resolves the edema. Untreated, vasogenic edema progresses to cytotoxic edema, infarction, or hemorrhage.

Wernicke Encephalopathy

Thiamine (B1) deficiency disrupts Krebs cycle enzyme function in the highest-metabolic regions. The three classic locations: mammillary bodies, periaqueductal gray matter, tectal plate. Clinical triad: ataxia (periaqueductal/vestibular), ophthalmoplegia (periaqueductal/oculomotor nuclei), memory impairment (mammillary bodies → Papez circuit). Mammillary body atrophy on sagittal MRI is a chronic sequela that supports prior Wernicke even when acute imaging was missed.

Marchiafava-Bignami Disease

Chronic alcoholism → demyelination and necrosis of the middle layers of the corpus callosum (genu and body), with two forms: type A (large lesions, callosal swelling, poor prognosis) and type B (smaller, better outcome). Diffusion restriction in the acute phase on the DWI. Not to be confused with Susac’s “snowball” — the layer specificity (middle layers only) and the clinical context (alcoholism) are distinct.

Radiation Leukoencephalopathy

Occurs often months/years after radiation therapy, can show central necrosis with surrounding enhancement (ring-like) and can pose diagnostic dilemma with recurring malignancy as differential.

The Big Picture

The paper’s broader lesson is that the pathologic substrate is the key to imaging.

  • Oligodendrocyte attack → white matter, U-fibers included.
  • Microglia infection → central, symmetric, atrophy.
  • Astrocyte targeting at periependymal sites → periependymal distribution, no U-fibers.
  • Vascular endzone disease → deep WM, U-fibers spared.
  • Osmotic/metabolic injury → anatomy of highest metabolic demand or least autoregulatory protection.

Once you understand why a lesion looks a certain way, the imaging becomes predictable rather than a list to memorize.

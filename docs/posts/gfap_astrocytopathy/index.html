GFAP astrocytopathy was only described in 2016. Which means it’s new enough that nobody has agreed on diagnostic criteria yet, the literature is thin, and it keeps getting mistaken for half a dozen other diseases. It presents as encephalomyelitis, fools people into thinking it’s an infection or a lymphoma, melts away dramatically with steroids, and then sometimes comes back for an encore. Once you know its imaging signature, though, you can pick it up early — before the serology returns, sometimes before anyone’s even thought to send the right test.

What Is GFAP?

Glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP) is the main structural scaffolding protein of mature astrocytes — the intermediate filament that gives them their shape and stops them from collapsing under mechanical stress. One critical detail: it sits inside the cell, not on the surface. This will matter enormously in about two paragraphs.

GFAP expression peaks in two specific places: perivascular astrocytic end-feet (the projections that sleeve around blood vessels and help form the blood-brain barrier) and ependymal astrocytes lining the central canal of the spinal cord. Not random — and not coincidental. These two locations map directly onto the two most characteristic imaging findings of the disease. The protein’s address is the lesion’s address.

Pathophysiology: Antibodies as Bystanders

Here’s where the intracellular location becomes important. Because GFAP is tucked away inside a living cell, the autoantibodies against it can’t actually reach their target. GFAP-IgG antibodies are not the weapon — they are the fingerprint. The real damage is done by CD8+ cytotoxic T cells. The antibodies are a biomarker of that process, not the cause of it. A positive titer tells you something autoimmune is happening; it doesn’t tell you the mechanism.

As for what starts the whole thing: cancer, in 20–34% of cases — most commonly an ovarian teratoma. Also EBV, herpes simplex encephalitis, traumatic brain injury, COVID-19, and dengue. Which means finding GFAP astrocytopathy should trigger a tumour screen, full stop.

The disease also likes company. Coexisting autoimmune conditions (type 1 diabetes, thyroid disease, rheumatoid arthritis) appear in around 20% of patients. Coexisting autoantibodies — NMDAR-IgG (22%) and AQP4-IgG — show up in up to 40%. If a patient is triple-positive for GFAP, NMDAR, and AQP4, the probability of an ovarian teratoma goes up substantially.

Clinical Presentation

The textbook presentation is acute or subacute meningoencephalomyelitis: fever, headache, neck stiffness, encephalopathy, seizures, psychiatric symptoms. It looks like bacterial meningitis. The CSF confirms the resemblance — lymphocyte-predominant pleocytosis, elevated protein, low glucose — and that’s usually the moment the patient gets transferred to the infectious diseases team. The neurology team gets called later, sometimes much later.

For the eyes, the characteristic finding is papillitis — bilateral optic disc oedema — not the painful retrobulbar optic neuritis of MS or NMOSD. The distinction matters clinically: papillitis is associated with a higher relapse risk and tends to require prolonged immunosuppression.

Rarer presentations include Parkinson-like features, rapidly progressive dementia, a chronic relapsing course, and an area postrema-like syndrome with cord lesions and no medullary involvement — which will make you think NMOSD right up until the serology comes back.

Neuroimaging: The Radial Pattern and Its Variants

One important calibration before starting: MRI is abnormal in only 44–66% of patients. Roughly a third of people with GFAP astrocytopathy will have an unremarkable scan. The implication is that if the clinical picture fits, you send the CSF regardless.

Brain

The pathognomonic finding is linear perivascular enhancement radiating outward from the ventricles, perpendicular to the ventricular surface, extending into the white matter. It’s the imaging correlate of perivascular lymphocytic infiltrates on histopathology. It appears in about 44% of patients — which makes it the most specific finding, even if it isn’t universal.

On T2/FLAIR, periventricular hyperintensities can spread to the brainstem, posterior thalamus bilaterally, basal ganglia, and limbic structures — wherever GFAP expression is highest. Enhancement morphology beyond the classic radial pattern varies considerably: punctate, nodular, leptomeningeal, ependymal, cranial nerve, serpentine, and even tumefactive-like patterns have all been reported. Flexibility is not always a virtue in differential diagnosis.

DWI and SWI are reassuringly boring — no true restricted diffusion (only T2 shine-through in the periventricular white matter), no microbleeds, no iron. The rare exception is restricted diffusion in the splenium of the corpus callosum.

Spinal Cord

Myelitis can dominate the clinical picture. Cord MRI shows longitudinally extensive T2 hyperintensity spanning ≥3 vertebral segments in around 50% of cases, mostly cervical and thoracic. Central grey matter involvement along the central canal is present in all cases of GFAP-related myelitis — exactly where you’d expect it, given the ependymal astrocyte distribution. Enhancement is linear pericentral, leptomeningeal, or patchy intramedullary.

Optic Pathway

Papillitis — bilateral optic disc head enhancement with surrounding oedema — is the dominant visual imaging finding. The posterior optic nerve and chiasm involvement that characterises NMOSD is not what you’ll see here. OCT can quantify the disc oedema through increased peripapillary retinal nerve fibre layer thickness, which is a handy noninvasive way to track the optic component without repeated gadolinium injections.

Diagnosis: CSF Over Serum, Cell-Based Assay Over IFA

GFAP-IgG in CSF detected by cell-based assay is near 100% specific. Serum testing is less reliable in both directions — use it as a trigger for further workup, not as a stand-alone confirmation.

The assay method is not a minor technical footnote: indirect immunofluorescence (IFA) can miss true cases that cell-based assay catches. A negative IFA does not rule out the diagnosis when the clinical and imaging picture is suggestive. Cell-based assay is the standard to insist on.

Two CSF biomarkers worth knowing about, even if they’re not yet in routine use: S100B and neurofilament light chain (NfL) are significantly elevated in GFAP astrocytopathy and may help distinguish it from MS, NMOSD, and varicella zoster meningitis. Promising, but still in development as clinical tools.

Treatment and Prognosis

IV methylprednisolone, and often fast. The steroid response is characteristic — dramatic enough in some cases that clinical improvement begins within the first treatment cycle. The course is monophasic in approximately 83% of patients. The remaining 20% relapse and need steroid-sparing maintenance: rituximab, mycophenolate, or azathioprine.

A useful radiological correlate of response: meningeal enhancement drops off before T2 signal in the white matter and cord normalises. So if you’re monitoring treatment on serial MRI, a clean meninges on the post-contrast sequence is a good early sign even before the parenchymal changes have resolved.

MRA and digital subtraction angiography are normal — which is not just a negative finding but actively useful for taking vasculitis off the table.

Radiographic Mimics

This is where everything from the previous sessions earns its keep.

NMOSD and MOGAD

Both share longitudinally extensive myelitis and optic pathway involvement — the most important inflammatory mimics.

The key distinction from NMOSD comes down to direction: NMOSD lesions line the ventricles (periependymal); GFAP lesions radiate outward from them. When NMOSD enhances, it’s cloud-like or pencil-thin periependymal — not spokes on a wheel.

Intravascular Large B Cell Lymphoma

The one you really cannot afford to miss. Lymphoma is rapidly fatal without chemotherapy, responds transiently to steroids (which creates a false impression of a demyelinating response), and can look alarmingly similar on MRI. The tell is restricted diffusion: intravascular lymphoma produces multifocal infarct-like lesions with genuinely low ADC values, from vessel occlusion by neoplastic cells. GFAP astrocytopathy never truly restricts — T2 shine-through only. When in doubt, biopsy before steroids: histology shows CD20-positive, PAX5-positive large B cells plugging vessel lumens, which is a completely different picture from the perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate of GFAP astrocytopathy.

CLIPPERS and SLIPPERS

CLIPPERS (chronic lymphocytic inflammation with pontine perivascular enhancement responsive to steroids) gives you “pepper-like” curvilinear pontine enhancement — the key being that it’s primarily infratentorial, while GFAP astrocytopathy is periventricular. SLIPPERS is the supratentorial version, which is harder to distinguish when the enhancement is radial. No specific serology for either. The absence of GFAP-IgG in CSF, plus the pontocerebellar predilection of CLIPPERS, is the clearest way through the differential.

Neurosarcoidosis

A perennial entry in every neuroradiology differential. Perivascular extension of leptomeningeal disease along Virchow-Robin spaces can produce radial periventricular enhancement that looks remarkably like GFAP astrocytopathy. Favouring sarcoidosis: nodular leptomeningeal enhancement clustering around the base and suprasellar region, cranial nerve involvement (especially facial and optic), hypothalamic-pituitary infiltration, and — often the most useful clue — known pulmonary sarcoidosis.

Primary Angiitis of the CNS

Multifocal enhancing lesions, T2 hyperintensities, and a patient who is clearly unwell — superficially similar. The definitive test is digital subtraction angiography: PACNS shows multifocal beading of small and medium vessels; GFAP astrocytopathy is angiographically clean. Additional favourable features for vasculitis: multiple infarcts in different vascular territories, microhaemorrhages on SWI, vessel wall enhancement on high-resolution MRI.

The Big Picture

GFAP astrocytopathy follows its protein’s address. GFAP lives in perivascular end-feet and the central canal — so the disease attacks perivascular spaces and the central cord. The antibodies didn’t do it; the T cells did. The antibodies just left their fingerprints at the scene.

Clinically, the disease is a triple mimic: it looks like infection (fever, meningism, bad CSF), it looks like NMOSD (LETM, optic involvement), and it looks like lymphoma (perivascular enhancement, steroid response). The imaging finding that breaks the deadlock is the radial perivascular enhancement perpendicular to the ventricles — a pattern that doesn’t fit anywhere else. When you see it, the next two steps are non-negotiable: CSF for GFAP-IgG, and a scan looking for ovarian teratoma.
